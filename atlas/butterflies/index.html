Butterflies & Moths

Beautiful pollinators in steep decline; native plants and pesticide-free gardens bring them back fast.

← The Atlas
Least ConcernVulnerableExtinct

The picture

Butterflies and moths pollinate wildflowers and feed countless birds - and their caterpillars are the base of many food webs. Populations have dropped sharply, but planting native host plants and leaving wild patches produces visible results within a single season.

The science

Order Lepidoptera, ~180,000 species. Most caterpillars are specialists - monarchs, for example, can only develop on milkweed, so losing one plant can mean losing the butterfly.

What they face

Loss of host plants

Tidy lawns and herbicides wipe out the milkweed and wildflowers caterpillars need.

Xerces Society

Pesticides

Insecticides kill caterpillars and the adults that feed on treated flowers.

Xerces Society

What is already working

Plant native host plants

Milkweed and local wildflowers feed caterpillars and adults.

Leave wild patches

Un-mowed corners give eggs and chrysalises a place to survive.

Reasons for hope

Monarch highways

Roadside and garden milkweed corridors are being planted across North America.

Gardens fill up fast

A patch of native flowers can draw butterflies back within weeks.
