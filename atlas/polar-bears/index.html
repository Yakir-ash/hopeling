The Arctic

Polar bears hunt seals from sea ice, so their future is tied to how much ice the Arctic keeps.

← The Atlas

The picture

The polar bear is the largest land carnivore on Earth and the living symbol of the Arctic. It hunts seals from the surface of frozen sea ice, so the length of the ice season decides how well a bear eats each year. They are not doomed: the world already saved them once, when nations agreed in 1973 to end unregulated hunting and numbers recovered. What they need now is cooler air, which makes the everyday climate actions in this app their most direct lifeline.

The science

Ursus maritimus, meaning 'sea bear'. Their fur is not white at all: each hair is a hollow, transparent tube that scatters light, and the skin beneath is black to absorb the sun. A thick fat layer keeps them warm in near-freezing water, and they can smell a seal through a metre of snow.

What they face

Shrinking sea ice

Bears hunt seals from the ice, so shorter ice seasons mean fewer chances to eat and store fat.

IUCN

More time on land

As ice retreats, bears spend longer ashore, where food is poor and encounters with people rise.

Polar Bears International

What is already working

Bear-safe communities

Patrols, bear-proof food storage and warning systems keep both people and bears alive in Arctic towns.

Climate action

Every tonne of emissions avoided is ice kept in the Arctic, the single thing that decides their future.

Reasons for hope

Nations acted before, and it worked

After the 1973 international agreement ended unregulated hunting, polar bear numbers recovered strongly. Countries can act together for a species.

Several populations are holding

Of the 19 tracked populations, a number are stable or increasing, and maternity dens are better protected than ever.
